## Guiding picture

The formalism has three layers: 

$$

\boxed{\text{fermionic Fock space } \mathcal F_-}
\qquad
\boxed{\text{Grassmann algebra } \mathcal G}
\qquad
\boxed{\text{extended graded space } \mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-}.

$$

The physical fermionic Fock space $\mathcal F_-$ contains ordinary fermion states: the vacuum, one-particle states, two-particle antisymmetric states, and so on. The Grassmann algebra $\mathcal G$ contains formal anticommuting generators such as $\eta_i$ and $\bar\eta_i$. A fermionic coherent state is not an ordinary vector in $\mathcal F_-$. It is a Grassmann-valued vector, i.e. an element of something like 

$$

\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-.

$$

 The hat on $\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}$ means that both factors carry *parity*, and whenever two odd objects cross, one inserts a minus sign.

The most important formula in the whole note is 

$$

(A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B)(C\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}D)
=
(-1)^{\lvert B\rvert\lvert C\rvert}
(AC\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BD),
$$

 for homogeneous operators or algebra elements. This is the Koszul sign rule.

## Ordinary vector spaces and tensor products

We work over $\mathbb C$, although almost everything below works over any field of characteristic not equal to $2$.


**Definition 1** (Vector space). A complex vector space $V$ is a set equipped with addition and scalar multiplication by complex numbers, satisfying the usual linear axioms.

**Definition 2** (Linear map). A map $T:V\to W$ is linear if 

$$

T(\alpha v+\beta w)=\alpha T(v)+\beta T(w)

$$

 for all $v,w\in V$ and $\alpha,\beta\in \mathbb C$.

**Definition 3** (Ordinary tensor product). The tensor product $V\otimes W$ is the vector space generated by symbols $v\otimes w$, subject to the bilinearity relations 

$$

\begin{align*}
(v_1+v_2)\otimes w &= v_1\otimes w+v_2\otimes w,\\
 v\otimes(w_1+w_2) &= v\otimes w_1+v\otimes w_2,\\
(\lambda v)\otimes w &= v\otimes(\lambda w)=\lambda(v\otimes w).
\end{align*}

$$
If $A:V\to V'$ and $B:W\to W'$ are ordinary linear maps, the ordinary tensor product map 

$$

A\otimes B:V\otimes W\to V'\otimes W'

$$

 is defined by 

$$

(A\otimes B)(v\otimes w)=Av\otimes Bw.

$$

 No signs occur here. Signs enter only after we add parity.

## Parity and super vector spaces

### The group Z2

The symbol $\mathbb Z_2$ means the two-element additive group 

$$

\mathbb Z_2=\{\bar 0,\bar 1\},

$$

 with addition modulo $2$. In formulas we usually write $0$ and $1$ instead of $\bar 0$ and $\bar 1$. Thus 

$$

1+1=0 \pmod 2.

$$

### What parity is


**Definition 4** ($\mathbb Z_2$-graded vector space, or super vector space). A $\mathbb Z_2$-graded vector space is a vector space $V$ together with a direct-sum decomposition 

$$

V=V_{\bar 0}\oplus V_{\bar 1}.

$$

 Elements of $V_{\bar 0}$ are called *even*. Elements of $V_{\bar 1}$ are called *odd*.

**Definition 5** (Homogeneous element and parity value). A vector $v\in V$ is called homogeneous if $v\in V_{\bar 0}$ or $v\in V_{\bar 1}$. For a nonzero homogeneous vector, its parity is the value 

$$

\lvert v\rvert\in \mathbb Z_2

$$

 defined by 

$$

\lvert v\rvert=0 \quad \text{if } v\in V_{\bar 0},
    \qquad
    \lvert v\rvert=1 \quad \text{if } v\in V_{\bar 1}.

$$

 Thus the parity function is not a linear map on all of $V$. More precisely, it is a function 

$$

\lvert \cdot\rvert: V_{\mathrm{hom}}\setminus\{0\}\longrightarrow \mathbb Z_2,

$$

 where $V_{\mathrm{hom}}=(V_{\bar 0}\cup V_{\bar 1})$ is the set of homogeneous vectors.

**Warning 6** (Not every vector has a parity). If $v_0\in V_{\bar 0}$ and $v_1\in V_{\bar 1}$ are both nonzero, then 

$$

v=v_0+v_1

$$

 is generally not homogeneous. It is not correct to assign a single parity $\lvert v\rvert$ to such a sum.
There is, however, a genuine linear operator associated with parity.


**Definition 7** (Parity operator). The parity operator on a super vector space $V=V_{\bar 0}\oplus V_{\bar 1}$ is the linear map 

$$

\Pi_V:V\to V

$$

 defined by 

$$

\Pi_V(v_0+v_1)=v_0-v_1,
    \qquad v_0\in V_{\bar 0},\; v_1\in V_{\bar 1}.

$$

 Equivalently, $\Pi_V$ has eigenvalue $+1$ on $V_{\bar 0}$ and eigenvalue $-1$ on $V_{\bar 1}$.
Thus there are two related but different notions:

        object                                  type                                             meaning
  ------------------ ---------------------------------------------------------- ------------------------------------------
   $\lvert v\rvert$   function on homogeneous nonzero vectors to $\mathbb Z_2$              even or odd label
       $\Pi_V$                          linear map $V\to V$                      parity operator with eigenvalues $\pm 1$
## Graded linear maps and operator parity


**Definition 8** (Homogeneous linear map). Let $V=V_{\bar 0}\oplus V_{\bar 1}$ and $W=W_{\bar 0}\oplus W_{\bar 1}$. A linear map $T:V\to W$ is homogeneous of degree $\alpha\in\mathbb Z_2$ if 

$$

T(V_i)\subseteq W_{i+\alpha}
    \qquad \text{for } i\in\mathbb Z_2.

$$

 If $\alpha=0$, $T$ is even. If $\alpha=1$, $T$ is odd.
So an even operator preserves parity, and an odd operator flips parity: 

$$

\begin{align*}
T\text{ even} &: V_{\bar 0}\to W_{\bar 0},\quad V_{\bar 1}\to W_{\bar 1},\\
T\text{ odd} &: V_{\bar 0}\to W_{\bar 1},\quad V_{\bar 1}\to W_{\bar 0}.
\end{align*}

$$

 For a homogeneous linear map, we write $\lvert T\rvert\in\mathbb Z_2$ for its degree.


**Example 9**. Let $V=\mathrm{span}\{e_0,e_1\}$, with $e_0$ even and $e_1$ odd. The operator 

$$

T e_0=e_1,
    \qquad
    T e_1=e_0

$$

 is odd. The operator 

$$

S e_0=2e_0,
    \qquad
    S e_1=-e_1

$$

 is even.
The space $\mathrm{Hom}(V,W)$ itself becomes graded: 

$$

\mathrm{Hom}(V,W)_{\bar\alpha}
    =
    \{T:V\to W\mid T(V_i)\subseteq W_{i+\alpha}\}.

$$

 In particular, $\mathrm{End}(V)=\mathrm{Hom}(V,V)$ is a graded algebra under composition.

## The graded tensor product of spaces


**Definition 10** (Graded tensor product of super vector spaces). Let 

$$

V=V_{\bar 0}\oplus V_{\bar 1},
    \qquad
    W=W_{\bar 0}\oplus W_{\bar 1}

$$

 be super vector spaces. Their graded tensor product 

$$

V\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}W

$$

 is the ordinary tensor product vector space $V\otimes W$, equipped with the grading 

$$

(V\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}W)_{\bar k}
    =
    \bigoplus_{i+j=k\;\mathrm{mod}\;2} V_{\bar i}\otimes W_{\bar j}.

$$

 Equivalently, for homogeneous pure tensors, 

$$

\lvert v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w\rvert=\lvert v\rvert+\lvert w\rvert\pmod 2.

$$
This definition is important because it says: as a vector space, 

$$

V\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}W = V\otimes W.

$$

 The new structure is the grading. The hat reminds us that this tensor product lives in the category of super vector spaces, where signs appear in exchanges and operator products.


**Example 11**. Suppose $v$ is odd and $w$ is odd. Then 

$$

\lvert v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w\rvert=1+1=0\pmod 2.

$$

 So an odd tensor an odd vector is even.
## The source of the minus sign: graded symmetry

In ordinary vector spaces, the flip map is 

$$

v\otimes w\longmapsto w\otimes v.

$$

 In super vector spaces, the natural flip map is not the ordinary flip. It is the graded flip.


**Definition 12** (Graded flip, or braiding). For homogeneous $v\in V$, $w\in W$, define 

$$

\tau_{V,W}(v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w)
    =
    (-1)^{\lvert v\rvert\lvert w\rvert}
    w\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}v.

$$

 Extend linearly to all of $V\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}W$.
Therefore: 

$$

\begin{align*}
\text{even crosses anything} &\quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{no sign},\\
\text{odd crosses odd} &\quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{minus sign}.
\end{align*}

$$

 This is the basic Koszul principle.

## Graded tensor product of operators

The graded tensor product of spaces tells us the parity of $v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w$. To do calculations, we also need to know how tensor products of operators act.


**Definition 13** (Action of a tensor product of homogeneous operators). Let $A:V\to V'$ and $B:W\to W'$ be homogeneous linear maps. Their graded tensor product operator 

$$

A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B:V\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}W\to V'\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}W'

$$

 is defined on homogeneous pure tensors by 

$$

(A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B)(v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w)
    =
    (-1)^{\lvert B\rvert\lvert v\rvert}
    Av\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}Bw.
$$

 Then extend linearly.
Why does $\lvert B\rvert\lvert v\rvert$ appear? Intuitively, the operator $B$, which acts on the right factor, must pass the left vector $v$ before reaching $w$. If both are odd, a minus sign appears.


**Warning 14**. Some books choose a slightly different convention for tensor products of maps. The convention in [\[eq:operator-action\]](#eq:operator-action) is the standard one compatible with the Koszul product rule [\[eq:koszul-algebra\]](#eq:koszul-algebra). Once a convention is chosen, it must be used consistently.

**Proposition 15** (Composition rule). *For homogeneous maps $A,B,C,D$ with compatible domains and codomains, 

$$

(A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B)(C\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}D)
    =
    (-1)^{\lvert B\rvert\lvert C\rvert}
    (AC\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BD).

$$

*
*Proof.* Apply the left-hand side to a homogeneous tensor $v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w$: 

$$

\begin{align*}
(C\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}D)(v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w)
&=
(-1)^{\lvert D\rvert\lvert v\rvert}
Cv\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}Dw.
\end{align*}

$$

 Now apply $A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B$: 

$$

\begin{align*}
(A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B)(Cv\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}Dw)
&=
(-1)^{\lvert B\rvert\lvert Cv\rvert}
ACv\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BDw \\
&=
(-1)^{\lvert B\rvert(\lvert C\rvert+\lvert v\rvert)}
ACv\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BDw.
\end{align*}

$$

 The total sign is 

$$

(-1)^{\lvert D\rvert\lvert v\rvert+\lvert B\rvert\lvert C\rvert+\lvert B\rvert\lvert v\rvert}.

$$

 On the other hand, $AC\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BD$ acting on $v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w$ gives the sign 

$$

(-1)^{(\lvert B\rvert+\lvert D\rvert)\lvert v\rvert}.

$$

 Thus the left-hand side equals 

$$

(-1)^{\lvert B\rvert\lvert C\rvert}(AC\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BD)(v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w).

$$

## Graded tensor product of algebras

A super algebra is an algebra $A=A_{\bar 0}\oplus A_{\bar 1}$ whose product respects parity: 

$$

A_iA_j\subseteq A_{i+j}.

$$


**Definition 16** (Graded tensor product of super algebras). If $A$ and $B$ are super algebras, their graded tensor product algebra $A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B$ has underlying vector space $A\otimes B$, grading as above, and multiplication 

$$

(a\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}b)(a'\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}b')
    =
    (-1)^{\lvert b\rvert\lvert a'\rvert}
    aa'\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}bb'
$$

 for homogeneous $a,a'\in A$, $b,b'\in B$.
This is the same sign rule as for operators. The middle objects $b$ and $a'$ must be exchanged to collect the $A$-parts together and the $B$-parts together.

## Exterior algebra

Before defining fermionic Fock space, we need exterior powers.


**Definition 17** (Exterior power). Let $\mathfrak h$ be a complex vector space. The $n$-th exterior power $\Lambda^n\mathfrak h$ is the vector space spanned by formal symbols 

$$

f_1\wedge\cdots\wedge f_n,
    \qquad f_i\in\mathfrak h,

$$

 subject to multilinearity and antisymmetry: 

$$

f_1\wedge\cdots\wedge f_i\wedge f_{i+1}\wedge\cdots\wedge f_n
    =
    -
    f_1\wedge\cdots\wedge f_{i+1}\wedge f_i\wedge\cdots\wedge f_n.

$$

 Also $\Lambda^0\mathfrak h=\mathbb C$.
The antisymmetry implies 

$$

f\wedge f=0.

$$

 This is the algebraic origin of the Pauli exclusion principle.


**Definition 18** (Exterior algebra). The algebraic exterior algebra of $\mathfrak h$ is 

$$

\Lambda \mathfrak h
    =
    \bigoplus_{n\ge 0}^{\mathrm{alg}}\Lambda^n\mathfrak h.

$$

 The superscript $\mathrm{alg}$ means algebraic direct sum: each element has only finitely many nonzero degree components.
If $\dim \mathfrak h=N<\infty$, then 

$$

\Lambda^n\mathfrak h=0 \quad \text{for } n>N,

$$

 so 

$$

\Lambda\mathfrak h=\bigoplus_{n=0}^{N}\Lambda^n\mathfrak h.

$$

 If $\mathfrak h$ is infinite-dimensional, then $\Lambda^n\mathfrak h$ is nonzero for every finite $n$. However, the algebraic exterior algebra still contains only finite-degree sums. It does not contain actual infinite-degree wedge monomials such as 

$$

e_1\wedge e_2\wedge e_3\wedge\cdots

$$

 unless one introduces a different construction, such as an infinite wedge space. That is not the standard fermionic Fock space used for finite-particle sectors.

## Fermionic Fock space

### One-particle space

The starting input is a one-particle Hilbert space $\mathfrak h$. For example:

- for $N$ discrete modes, $\mathfrak h=\mathbb C^N$;

- for particles on a spatial domain, $\mathfrak h$ may be an $L^2$-space with spin labels.

### Algebraic fermionic Fock space


**Definition 19** (Algebraic fermionic Fock space). The algebraic fermionic Fock space over $\mathfrak h$ is 

$$

\mathcal F_-^{\mathrm{alg}}(\mathfrak h)
    =
    \bigoplus_{n=0}^{\infty,\mathrm{alg}}\Lambda^n\mathfrak h.

$$

 The vector $1\in \Lambda^0\mathfrak h=\mathbb C$ is called the vacuum and is usually denoted by 

$$

\lvert 0\rangle\quad\text{or}\quad \Omega.

$$
An element of $\Lambda^n\mathfrak h$ is an $n$-fermion vector. The word "fermionic" means the $n$-particle wavefunction is antisymmetric.

### Hilbert fermionic Fock space

If $\mathfrak h$ is a Hilbert space, each $\Lambda^n\mathfrak h$ inherits a Hilbert-space inner product. The physical Hilbert Fock space is the Hilbert direct sum 

$$

\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)=\bigoplus_{n=0}^{\infty}\Lambda^n\mathfrak h,

$$

 where $\oplus$ denotes the Hilbert direct sum. Concretely, a vector $\Psi\in\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)$ is a sequence 

$$

\Psi=(\psi_0,\psi_1,\psi_2,\ldots),
    \qquad \psi_n\in\Lambda^n\mathfrak h,

$$

 with 

$$

\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\|\psi_n\|^2<\infty.

$$


**Warning 20** (No infinite wedge degree in standard Fock space). Even when $\mathfrak h$ is infinite-dimensional, standard Fock space does not contain one component of degree $\infty$. It contains components of degree $n=0,1,2,\ldots$, each finite. The Hilbert completion allows infinitely many finite-particle components to appear in a square-summable sequence.
### Finite number of modes

If $\mathfrak h=\mathbb C^N$, then 

$$

\mathcal F_-(\mathbb C^N)=\bigoplus_{n=0}^N\Lambda^n\mathbb C^N.

$$

 This has dimension $2^N$. If $e_1,\\ldots,e_N$ is an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb C^N$, a basis of $\mathcal F_-(\mathbb C^N)$ is 

$$

e_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge e_{i_k},
    \qquad
    1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le N.

$$

 In occupation-number notation, this is written as 

$$

\lvert n_1,\ldots,n_N\rangle,
    \qquad n_i\in\{0,1\}.

$$

 The fact that $n_i$ is either $0$ or $1$ is exactly the fact that $e_i\wedge e_i=0$.

## Creation and annihilation operators

Let $\mathfrak h$ be a one-particle Hilbert space. We use the physics convention that the inner product $\langle f,g\rangle$ is conjugate-linear in $f$ and linear in $g$.


**Definition 21** (Creation operator). For $f\in\mathfrak h$, the fermionic creation operator $a^\dagger(f)$ is defined on wedge monomials by 

$$

a^\dagger(f)(g_1\wedge\cdots\wedge g_n)
    =
    f\wedge g_1\wedge\cdots\wedge g_n.

$$

 In particular, 

$$

a^\dagger(f)\Omega=f.

$$

**Definition 22** (Annihilation operator). The annihilation operator $a(f)$ is the adjoint of $a^\dagger(f)$. On wedge monomials it is given by 

$$

a(f)(g_1\wedge\cdots\wedge g_n)
    =
    \sum_{k=1}^n (-1)^{k-1}\langle f,g_k\rangle
g_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \widehat{g_k}\wedge\cdots\wedge g_n,

$$

 where the hat over $g_k$ means that $g_k$ is omitted. Also 

$$

a(f)\Omega=0.

$$
For an orthonormal basis $e_i$, write 

$$

a_i=a(e_i),
    \qquad
    a_i^\dagger=a^\dagger(e_i).

$$

 Then 

$$

a_i^\dagger\Omega=e_i,
    \qquad
    a_i\Omega=0.

$$


**Proposition 23** (Canonical anticommutation relations). *The creation and annihilation operators satisfy 

$$

\{a(f),a^\dagger(g)\}=\langle f,g\rangle I,

$$

 

$$

\{a(f),a(g)\}=0,
    \qquad
    \{a^\dagger(f),a^\dagger(g)\}=0.

$$

 In mode notation, 

$$

\{a_i,a_j^\dagger\}=\delta_{ij}I,
    \qquad
    \{a_i,a_j\}=0,
    \qquad
    \{a_i^\dagger,a_j^\dagger\}=0.

$$

*
Here $\{X,Y\}=XY+YX$.

## Parity on fermionic Fock space

The fermionic Fock space is automatically graded by particle-number parity.


**Definition 24** (Number operator). The number operator $\mathcal N$ is defined by 

$$

\mathcal N\psi=n\psi
    \qquad \text{for } \psi\in\Lambda^n\mathfrak h.

$$

**Definition 25** (Fermion parity operator). The fermion parity operator is 

$$

\Pi_{\mathcal F}=(-1)^{\mathcal N}.

$$

 Thus 

$$

\Pi_{\mathcal F}\psi=(-1)^n\psi
    \qquad \text{if } \psi\in\Lambda^n\mathfrak h.

$$
Therefore 

$$

\mathcal F_{-,\bar 0}=\bigoplus_{\substack{n\ge0\\ n\text{ even}}}\Lambda^n\mathfrak h,
    \qquad
    \mathcal F_{-,\bar 1}=\bigoplus_{\substack{n\ge0\\ n\text{ odd}}}\Lambda^n\mathfrak h,

$$

 where both direct sums are Hilbert direct sums.

The vacuum is even: 

$$

\lvert 0\rangle\in\mathcal F_{-,\bar 0}.

$$

 A one-particle state is odd: 

$$

f\in\Lambda^1\mathfrak h\subset\mathcal F_{-,\bar 1}.

$$

 A two-particle state is even: 

$$

f\wedge g\in\Lambda^2\mathfrak h\subset\mathcal F_{-,\bar 0}.

$$

Creation and annihilation operators are odd: 

$$

\lvert a_i\rvert=1,
    \qquad
    \lvert a_i^\dagger\rvert=1.

$$

 Indeed, they change particle number by $\pm 1$, so they flip even states to odd states and odd states to even states.

## Grassmann algebras

### Definition

Let $E$ be a finite-dimensional complex vector space with basis 

$$

\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_m.

$$

 The Grassmann algebra generated by these symbols is the exterior algebra 

$$

\mathcal G=\Lambda E.

$$

 Thus $\mathcal G$ is spanned by monomials 

$$

\theta_{i_1}\theta_{i_2}\cdots\theta_{i_k},
    \qquad
    i_1<\cdots<i_k,

$$

 with multiplication determined by 

$$

\theta_i\theta_j=-\theta_j\theta_i,
    \qquad
    \theta_i^2=0.

$$


**Remark 26**. The multiplication in $\mathcal G$ is usually written by juxtaposition rather than $\wedge$. Algebraically, it is the same exterior product.
### Even and odd parts of a Grassmann algebra

The Grassmann algebra is graded by degree modulo $2$: 

$$

\mathcal G_{\bar 0}
    =
    \bigoplus_{k\text{ even}}\Lambda^k E,
    \qquad
    \mathcal G_{\bar 1}
    =
    \bigoplus_{k\text{ odd}}\Lambda^k E.

$$

 Thus 

$$

1,\; \theta_i\theta_j,\; \theta_i\theta_j\theta_k\theta_l

$$

 are even, while 

$$

\theta_i,\; \theta_i\theta_j\theta_k

$$

 are odd.

### Eta and eta-bar variables

In fermionic coherent-state calculus, one often uses pairs of generators 

$$

\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_N,
    \qquad
    \bar\eta_1,\ldots,\bar\eta_N.

$$

 Formally, these are independent basis vectors of an auxiliary vector space 

$$

E=\mathrm{span}_{\mathbb C}\{\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_N,\bar\eta_1,\ldots,\bar\eta_N\}.

$$

 Then 

$$

\mathcal G=\Lambda E.

$$

 The bar in $\bar\eta_i$ is a label indicating the generator used for the bra side of coherent-state notation. In the purely algebraic path-integral formalism, $\bar\eta_i$ is not forced to be the complex conjugate of $\eta_i$. The two are independent Grassmann generators.

## Berezin differentiation

Grassmann differentiation is not defined by a limit. There is no topology or order relation on a Grassmann algebra that would make such a limit meaningful. Instead, differentiation is defined algebraically.


**Definition 27** (Left Berezin derivative). Let $\mathcal G=\Lambda(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_m)$. The left derivative $\partial^L_i=\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_i}$ is the unique odd linear operator $\mathcal G\to\mathcal G$ such that 

$$

\partial^L_i(\theta_j)=\delta_{ij}

$$

 and satisfying the graded Leibniz rule 

$$

\partial^L_i(uv)
    =
    (\partial^L_i u)v
    +
    (-1)^{\lvert u\rvert}u(\partial^L_i v)
$$

 for homogeneous $u\in\mathcal G$.
The operator $\partial_i^L$ is odd because it lowers Grassmann degree by one.


**Example 28**. For two generators $\theta_1,\theta_2$, 

$$

\partial^L_1(\theta_1\theta_2)=\theta_2,

$$

 while 

$$

\partial^L_2(\theta_1\theta_2)
    =
    -\theta_1.

$$

 The second minus sign comes from the graded Leibniz rule because $\theta_1$ is odd.
For a monomial with distinct generators, 

$$

\partial^L_j(\theta_{i_1}\cdots\theta_{i_k})
    =
    \sum_{r: i_r=j}(-1)^{r-1}
    \theta_{i_1}\cdots \widehat{\theta_{i_r}}\cdots\theta_{i_k}.

$$

## Berezin integration

Berezin integration is also algebraic. It is coefficient extraction.


**Definition 29** (One Grassmann variable). For $\mathcal G=\Lambda(\theta)$, define 

$$

\int d\theta\;1=0,
    \qquad
    \int d\theta\;\theta=1.

$$

 Thus, for $f(\theta)=a+b\theta$, 

$$

\int d\theta\;f(\theta)=b.

$$
So, for one variable, 

$$

\int d\theta = \frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta}

$$

 as linear functionals.


**Definition 30** (Several variables, ordered convention). Fix an ordered list of generators 

$$

\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_m.

$$

 Define 

$$

\int d\theta_m\cdots d\theta_1\; \theta_1\cdots\theta_m=1,

$$

 and define the integral to be zero on all monomials not containing the full product $\theta_1\cdots\theta_m$. Equivalently, 

$$

\int d\theta_m\cdots d\theta_1
    =
    \partial_m^L\cdots\partial_1^L.

$$
The order of the differentials is part of the definition. Changing the order can change signs.


**Example 31**. With ordered variables $\theta_1,\theta_2$, 

$$

\int d\theta_2d\theta_1\;\theta_1\theta_2=1,

$$

 but 

$$

\int d\theta_2d\theta_1\;\theta_2\theta_1=-1.

$$
## The extended Grassmann-Fock space

Now we combine the Grassmann algebra and the fermionic Fock space.


**Definition 32** (Grassmann-extended Fock space). Let $\mathcal G$ be the Grassmann algebra generated by $\eta_i,\bar\eta_i$, and let $\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)$ be the fermionic Fock space. The Grassmann-extended Fock space is 

$$

\mathcal F_\mathcal G
    =
    \mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h).

$$

 As a vector space, this is $\mathcal G\otimes\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)$. As a super vector space, its parity is 

$$

\lvert g\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi\rvert
    =
    \lvert g\rvert+\lvert \psi\rvert\pmod 2

$$

 for homogeneous $g\in\mathcal G$, $\psi\in\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)$.
An element of $\mathcal F_\mathcal G$ has the form 

$$

\Psi=\sum_\alpha g_\alpha\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi_\alpha,

$$

 where $g_\alpha\in\mathcal G$ and $\psi_\alpha\in\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)$.


**Warning 33** (Not an ordinary physical Hilbert space). $\mathcal F_\mathcal G$ is not the physical Hilbert space of the fermionic system. It is a Grassmann-valued extension used as a calculational device. Fermionic coherent states live naturally in this extended space, not in the original physical Fock space $\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)$.
## How operators act on the Grassmann-extended Fock space

There are two kinds of basic operators on the extended space.

### Grassmann multiplication operators

For $\theta\in\mathcal G$, define left multiplication 

$$

M_\theta:\mathcal G\to\mathcal G,
    \qquad
    M_\theta(g)=\theta g.

$$

 If $\theta$ is odd, then $M_\theta$ is an odd operator. On $\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-$, it is represented as 

$$

M_\theta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I_{\mathcal F}.

$$

 Thus 

$$

(M_\theta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I)(g\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi)
    =
    \theta g\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi.

$$

 No sign appears here because the right operator is the identity, which is even.

### Fock operators

A Fock operator such as $a_i$ acts on the Fock factor. On $\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-$, it is represented as 

$$

I_\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a_i.

$$

 Since $a_i$ is odd, its action on a homogeneous tensor is 

$$

(I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a_i)(g\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi)
    =
    (-1)^{\lvert g\rvert}
    g\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a_i\psi.
$$

 This is just the general operator rule [\[eq:operator-action\]](#eq:operator-action).


**Example 34**. Let $\eta$ be odd and let $\lvert 1\rangle$ be a one-particle state. Then 

$$

(I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a)(\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 1\rangle)
    =
    -\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a\lvert 1\rangle.

$$

 The minus sign appears because $a$ is odd and $\eta$ is odd.
### Why Grassmann variables anticommute with fermionic operators

In the extended space, write 

$$

\eta := M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I,
    \qquad
    a := I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a.

$$

 Both are odd operators. Then 

$$

\begin{align*}
\eta a
&=(M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I)(I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a) \\
&=M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a,
\end{align*}

$$

 while 

$$

\begin{align*}
a\eta
&=(I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a)(M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I) \\
&=(-1)^{\lvert a\rvert\lvert M_\eta\rvert}
M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a \\
&=-M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a.
\end{align*}

$$

 Therefore 

$$

a\eta=-\eta a.

$$

 This is not an additional arbitrary rule. It follows from the graded tensor product.

## Berezin calculus on Grassmann-valued Fock vectors

Let 

$$

\Psi=\sum_\alpha g_\alpha\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi_\alpha
    \in \mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-.

$$

 A Berezin derivative acts on the Grassmann coefficient and leaves the Fock vector alone: 

$$

\frac{\partial^L}{\partial\theta_i}\Psi
    =
    \sum_\alpha
    \left(\frac{\partial^L g_\alpha}{\partial\theta_i}\right)
    \mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi_\alpha.

$$

 Equivalently, this is 

$$

\partial_i^L\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I_\mathcal F.

$$

 Since the operator on the right factor is $I_\mathcal F$, no extra Koszul sign appears in this coefficientwise action.

Similarly, 

$$

\int d\theta_i\;\Psi
    =
    \sum_\alpha
    \left(\int d\theta_i\;g_\alpha\right)
    \mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi_\alpha.

$$

 Thus one should not say that a Fock vector itself is differentiated with respect to a Grassmann variable. The precise statement is: the Grassmann-valued coefficient is differentiated, while the Fock vector is carried along unchanged.

## One-mode fermionic coherent states

Consider one fermionic mode. The Fock space is 

$$

\mathcal F=\mathrm{span}\{\lvert 0\rangle,\lvert 1\rangle\},
    \qquad
    \lvert 1\rangle=a^\dagger\lvert 0\rangle.

$$

 The parities are 

$$

\lvert \lvert 0\rangle\rvert=0,
    \qquad
    \lvert \lvert 1\rangle\rvert=1,
    \qquad
    \lvert a\rvert=\lvert a^\dagger\rvert=1.

$$

 Let $\mathcal G=\Lambda(\eta,\bar\eta)$, with $\eta$ and $\bar\eta$ independent odd generators.


**Definition 35** (One-mode ket coherent state). Define 

$$

\lvert \eta\rangle
    =
    1\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 0\rangle
    -
    \eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 1\rangle
    \in \mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F.

$$
This vector is even: the first term is even plus even, and the second term is odd plus odd.

Now compute $a\lvert \eta\rangle$, where $a$ means $I_\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a$: 

$$

\begin{align*}
    a\lvert \eta\rangle
    &=
    (I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a)(1\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 0\rangle)
    -
    (I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a)(\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 1\rangle) \\
    &=
    1\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a\lvert 0\rangle
    -
    \left((-1)^{\lvert \eta\rvert}\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a\lvert 1\rangle\right) \\
    &=
    0-
    \left(-\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 0\rangle\right) \\
    &=
    \eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 0\rangle.
\end{align*}

$$

 On the other hand, multiplication by $\eta$ gives 

$$

\begin{align*}
    \eta\lvert \eta\rangle
    &=
    (M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I)
    \left(1\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 0\rangle-\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 1\rangle\right) \\
    &=
    \eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 0\rangle-\eta^2\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 1\rangle \\
    &=
    \eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\lvert 0\rangle.
\end{align*}

$$

 Therefore 

$$

a\lvert \eta\rangle=\eta\lvert \eta\rangle.

$$

 This is an algebraic identity in $\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F$. It is not a statement that $a$ has an ordinary complex eigenvector in the physical Fock space.

## Exponential notation for coherent states

The same one-mode ket can be written as 

$$

\lvert \eta\rangle=e^{-\eta a^\dagger}\lvert 0\rangle.

$$

 This notation is finite because $(\eta a^\dagger)^2=0$. Indeed, 

$$

e^{-\eta a^\dagger}=1-\eta a^\dagger.

$$

 Acting on the vacuum gives 

$$

(1-\eta a^\dagger)\lvert 0\rangle
    =
    \lvert 0\rangle-\eta\lvert 1\rangle.

$$

 The product $\eta a^\dagger$ is even because it is odd times odd.

For $N$ modes, define 

$$

\lvert \eta\rangle
    =
    \exp\left(-\sum_{i=1}^N \eta_i a_i^\dagger\right)\lvert 0\rangle.

$$

 The operator $\sum_i\eta_i a_i^\dagger$ is even. With the graded sign conventions above, one obtains 

$$

a_i\lvert \eta\rangle=\eta_i\lvert \eta\rangle.

$$

## Bras, overlaps, and a one-mode resolution of identity

A fully precise treatment of bras requires a convention for graded duals. This note records the common physics convention for one mode, enough to see how the Berezin integral works.

Take 

$$

\langle \bar\eta\rvert
    =
    \langle 0\rvert-\langle 1\rvert\bar\eta,

$$

 where $\bar\eta$ is an independent Grassmann generator placed on the right of the bra coefficient. Then 

$$

\langle \bar\eta \mid \eta'\rangle
    =
    1+\bar\eta\eta'
    =
    e^{\bar\eta\eta'}.

$$

 For one pair $(\eta,\bar\eta)$, choose the integration convention 

$$

\int d\bar\eta\,d\eta\;\eta\bar\eta=1.

$$

 Equivalently, 

$$

\int d\bar\eta\,d\eta\;\bar\eta\eta=-1.

$$

 Then 

$$

e^{-\bar\eta\eta}=1-\bar\eta\eta=1+\eta\bar\eta.

$$

 With these conventions, 

$$

I_
    \mathcal F
    =
    \int d\bar\eta\,d\eta\;
    e^{-\bar\eta\eta}
    \lvert \eta\rangle\langle \bar\eta\rvert.

$$

 Let us verify this directly. First 

$$

\lvert \eta\rangle\langle \bar\eta\rvert
    =
    \lvert 0\rangle\langle 0\rvert
    -\lvert 0\rangle\langle 1\rvert\bar\eta
    -\eta\lvert 1\rangle\langle 0\rvert
    +\eta\lvert 1\rangle\langle 1\rvert\bar\eta.

$$

 Multiplying by $e^{-\bar\eta\eta}=1+\eta\bar\eta$, the only terms contributing to the Berezin integral are those proportional to $\eta\bar\eta$. The off-diagonal terms do not contain both variables after multiplication, because an extra repeated variable gives zero. Hence 

$$

\begin{align*}
\int d\bar\eta d\eta\;e^{-\bar\eta\eta}\lvert \eta\rangle\langle \bar\eta\rvert
&=
\lvert 0\rangle\langle 0\rvert
+
\lvert 1\rangle\langle 1\rvert \\
&=I_\mathcal F.
\end{align*}

$$


**Warning 36** (Conventions matter). If one changes the order of $d\eta$ and $d\bar\eta$, or changes signs in the definitions of $\lvert \eta\rangle$ and $\langle \bar\eta\rvert$, corresponding signs in the overlap and identity resolution change. The mathematics is not ambiguous once all conventions are fixed.
## What is essential and what is notation

The essential structures are these:

1.  A one-particle Hilbert space $\mathfrak h$.

2.  A fermionic Fock space 

$$

\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)=\bigoplus_{n=0}^\infty\Lambda^n\mathfrak h,

$$

 where $\oplus$ denotes the Hilbert direct sum. For $\dim\mathfrak h=N<\infty$, this stops at $n=N$.

3.  A parity decomposition 

$$

\mathcal F_-=(\mathcal F_-)_{\bar 0}\oplus(\mathcal F_-)_{\bar 1}

$$

 by even or odd particle number.

4.  An auxiliary Grassmann algebra 

$$

\mathcal G=\Lambda\mathrm{span}\{\eta_i,\bar\eta_i\}.

$$

5.  The extended super vector space 

$$

\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-.

$$

6.  The Koszul rule 

$$

(A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B)(C\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}D)
        =
        (-1)^{\lvert B\rvert\lvert C\rvert}
        AC\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BD.

$$

7.  Berezin differentiation and integration, defined as algebraic operations on $\mathcal G$, acting coefficientwise on $\mathcal G\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\mathcal F_-$.

## Common confusions resolved

### Is fermionic Fock space an exterior algebra?

For finitely many modes, yes: the fermionic Fock space is naturally 

$$

\mathcal F_-(\mathbb C^N)=\Lambda\mathbb C^N.

$$

 For an infinite-dimensional one-particle Hilbert space, the physical Hilbert Fock space is the Hilbert direct-sum completion 

$$

\mathcal F_-(\mathfrak h)=\widehat{\bigoplus}_{n=0}^\infty\Lambda^n\mathfrak h.

$$

 It is best thought of as the completed antisymmetric Fock space, built from exterior powers. The algebraic finite-particle subspace is the algebraic exterior algebra-like object 

$$

\mathcal F_-^{\mathrm{alg}}(\mathfrak h)=\bigoplus_{n=0}^{\infty,\mathrm{alg}}\Lambda^n\mathfrak h.

$$

### Does standard Fock space include infinite forms?

No. It includes finite $n$-particle sectors for all $n\ge 0$. A general Hilbert Fock vector may have infinitely many nonzero finite-particle components, but it is not a single infinite wedge monomial.

### What exactly is parity?

There are two precise meanings:

- the parity value $\lvert x\rvert\in\mathbb Z_2$, defined only for homogeneous nonzero elements or homogeneous operators;

- the parity operator $\Pi:V\to V$, a linear map with eigenvalue $+1$ on even vectors and $-1$ on odd vectors.

### What does the hat on the tensor product mean?

For spaces, $V\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}W$ is the ordinary tensor product vector space with the graded parity rule 

$$

\lvert v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w\rvert=\lvert v\rvert+\lvert w\rvert.

$$

 For operators or algebras, the hat also reminds us to use the Koszul multiplication rule 

$$

(A\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}B)(C\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}D)
    =
    (-1)^{\lvert B\rvert\lvert C\rvert}AC\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}BD.

$$

### What is being differentiated in Grassmann calculus?

Only the Grassmann coefficient is differentiated. If 

$$

\Psi=\sum_\alpha g_\alpha\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi_\alpha,

$$

 then 

$$

\partial_i\Psi=\sum_\alpha (\partial_i g_\alpha)\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}\psi_\alpha.

$$

 The Fock vector $\psi_\alpha$ is not itself differentiated with respect to $\eta_i$.

### Why does a eta equal minus eta a?

Because $a=I\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}a$ is an odd operator on the Fock factor, while $\eta=M_\eta\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}I$ is an odd multiplication operator on the Grassmann factor. The graded tensor product says odd operators from separate factors anticommute.

## Final summary

Fermionic coherent-state calculus is not mysterious once the bookkeeping is made explicit: 

$$

\boxed{\text{fermionic Fock space is built from exterior powers,}}

$$

 

$$

\boxed{\text{Grassmann variables are generators of another exterior algebra,}}

$$

 

$$

\boxed{\text{the two are combined by a graded tensor product,}}

$$

 

$$

\boxed{\text{and Berezin calculus is coefficient extraction on the Grassmann algebra.}}

$$

 The signs are not arbitrary. They are exactly the signs forced by the graded flip 

$$

v\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}w\longmapsto (-1)^{\lvert v\rvert\lvert w\rvert}w\mathbin{\widehat\otimes}v

$$

 and by the corresponding Koszul multiplication rule.
