I. From Photon Lagrangian to Quantized Resonator Modes

The free-space electromagnetic field is governed by the Lagrangian density $$ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{EM}} = \frac{\varepsilon_0}{2}\mathbf{E}^2 - \frac{1}{2\mu_0}\mathbf{B}^2 . $$ To model a long, two-conductor transmission line (coax, CPW, nanowire) we restrict to the transverse-electro-magnetic (TEM) sector, where $\mathbf{E}_\perp = -\nabla_\perp V$ and $\mathbf{B}_\perp = \nabla_\perp\times ( \hat{z}\, \Phi )$. Introducing the node flux $\Phi(z,t)=\!\int_{-\infty}^{t}\!V(z,t')dt'$, the field depends only on the longitudinal coordinate $z$ and time $t$. After integrating over the transverse cross-section we obtain the 1-D Lagrangian $$ \mathcal{L} = \int_0^{\ell}\!dz\; \Bigg[ \frac{C_\ell}{2}\bigl(\partial_t\Phi\bigr)^2 -\frac{1}{2L_\ell}\bigl(\partial_z\Phi\bigr)^2 \Bigg], $$ which is precisely the Lagrangian of the telegrapher’s equation with distributed capacitance $C_\ell$ and inductance $L_\ell$. Quantizing this field therefore yields photons that are confined standing-wave solutions of a microwave resonator.

For an open-ended $\lambda/2$ resonator (voltage antinodes at both ends), we expand $$ \Phi(z,t)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \varphi_n(t)\,\cos\!\bigl(k_n z\bigr),\qquad k_n=\frac{n\pi}{\ell}. $$ Canonical quantization gives $$ \varphi_n(t)= \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2\omega_n C_\ell\ell}} \bigl(a_n+ a_n^\dagger\bigr),\quad \omega_n = v_p k_n,\; v_p = \frac{1}{\sqrt{L_\ell C_\ell}} . $$ Each pair $(a_n,a_n^\dagger)$ obeys $[a_n,a_m^\dagger]=\delta_{nm}$ and corresponds to a single harmonic-oscillator mode—a photon—in the resonator.

II. Voltage & Current Operators, Nodes & Antinodes

Operator expressions follow directly from the mode expansion: $$ \hat V(z) = \partial_t\hat\Phi = i\sum_n \sqrt{\frac{\hbar\omega_n}{2 C_\ell\ell}} \bigl(a_n^\dagger- a_n\bigr)\cos(k_n z), $$ $$ \hat I(z) = -\frac{1}{L_\ell}\partial_z\hat\Phi = \sum_n \sqrt{\frac{\hbar\omega_n}{2 L_\ell\ell}} \bigl(a_n^\dagger+ a_n\bigr)\sin(k_n z). $$ Hence:

  • Voltage antinode ($\cos k_n z = \pm1$): $\hat V$ maximal, $\hat I=0$.
  • Current antinode ($\sin k_n z = \pm1$): $\hat I$ maximal, $\hat V=0$.
These relations underpin how qubits couple to the resonator field.

III. Spin-Qubit Coupling: Transverse vs. Longitudinal

For a double-dot spin qubit (e.g. $S$–$T_0$ qubit) positioned at $z=z_0$:

  • Transverse (electric-dipole) coupling
    Occurs when the qubit’s charge-like dipole couples to the local voltage: $$ H_\perp = g_\perp \,\hat\sigma_{x}\, \bigl(a + a^\dagger\bigr), $$ with $g_\perp \propto \partial\omega_q/\partial V \;\cos k_1 z_0$.
  • Longitudinal (dispersive) coupling
    Arises from flux or detuning shifts proportional to the current (magnetic field) or to charge‐state energy: $$ H_\parallel = g_\parallel \,\hat\sigma_{z}\, \bigl(a + a^\dagger\bigr),\qquad g_\parallel \propto \sin k_1 z_0. $$ A longitudinal term commutes with the bare qubit Hamiltonian and enables fast, high-fidelity gates via qubit-state–dependent phase-space displacements.
Choice of $z_0$ lets an experimentalist engineer predominantly transverse ($z_0\approx 0$) or longitudinal ($z_0\approx\ell/2$) coupling.

IV. Kinetic Inductance & Order-Parameter Anisotropy

In a superconductor, Cooper-pair inertia produces a kinetic inductance $$ L_k = \frac{m^\ast}{n_s q^{\,2}A}, $$ where $n_s(T)$ is the superfluid density. Within the two-fluid or Mattis-Bardeen picture, $n_s(T)$ decreases with temperature, so $L_k(T)\!\propto\!1/n_s(T)$ grows and the resonator frequency $f_r(T)\!\approx\!1/\bigl[2\pi\sqrt{(L_g+L_k)C}\bigr]$ shifts upward.

If the superconducting order parameter is anisotropic ($\Delta=\Delta(\mathbf{k})$), then the London penetration depth $\lambda_L(\hat{n})$ and thus $L_k(\hat{n})$ are direction dependent. Rotating a thin-film resonator relative to the crystal axes or measuring its frequency shift in an in-plane magnetic field can therefore map the anisotropy of $\Delta$.
